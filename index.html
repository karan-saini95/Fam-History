Generational timeline

Rup Lal Trehan (great-grandfather)
c. Early 1900s – c. 1950s
Indian Railways Officer
British Colonial Service
1900s
Sri Hargobindpur / Jalandhar, Punjab
Born into the Khatri (Trehan clan) community in Punjab's Majha belt — a region with deep Sikh institutional history. Guru Angad, the second Sikh Guru, belonged to the Trehan lineage. Khatris were traditionally literacy-based professionals: administrators, merchants, officers.
1930s
Indian Railways — British India
Joins as a rank officer in the colonial railway administration. The railways were the backbone of imperial logistics — his role required English proficiency, numeracy, and institutional trust. Such positions were commonly held by Khatris.
c.1940
Limbe (Victoria), British Cameroons — West Africa
Posted to the coastal port town of Limbe during the WWII era as part of the British Empire's deployment of skilled Indian personnel across its territories. Limbe was linked administratively to Nigeria and played a role in port logistics and resource movement.
1941
A son born in Cameroon
Your grandfather is born in Limbe — the family's single furthest departure from Punjab, and the only member of the line born outside India.
Late 1940s
Return to India
The family returns around Indian independence (1947) — part of the broad wind-down of colonial postings as the British Empire retreated.
Dr. Satya Paul Trehan (grandfather)
1941 – 2010
Indian Army Officer (~8 years)
Hindustan Motors, Calcutta
1941
Born in Limbe, British Cameroons
A direct product of his father's imperial posting — born on the West African coast, he would be raised in Punjab.
1950s
Ludhiana, Punjab
Raised in Ludhiana and attends Arya College — a well-regarded institution reflecting continued access to formal education within the family.
1960s
Indian Army — Delhi · Siliguri, West Bengal · Jalandhar, Punjab
Serves approximately 8 years as an officer during India's nation-building phase. Postings include Delhi, Siliguri (strategically vital, near the Northeast Corridor), and Jalandhar — a breadth of deployment that mirrors his father's wide-ranging service.
1970s
Calcutta, West Bengal
Takes voluntary exit from the army and joins Hindustan Motors, one of India's premier manufacturers, known for the Ambassador car. This marks the family's shift from public/military service to India's early industrial economy. He meets his wife here.
Ambika Trehan (mother)
1973 – present
Lecturer of Biology
Chandigarh · Panjab University
1973
Born in Fazilka, Punjab
Born at her maternal grandmother's home near the India–Pakistan border — a common practice of the time, returning to family for childbirth. The family is otherwise based in Calcutta.
1973–76
Calcutta, West Bengal
Early childhood in the city of her parents' professional life — Bengal at a significant moment in post-independence history.
1976–83
Valsad, Gujarat
Seven formative years in western India — the family moves from East to West, from Calcutta's Bengali culture to Gujarat's coastal belt. A significant stretch that shapes early life.
1983–86
Faridabad, Haryana
The family moves to the NCR's industrial belt — signaling a return northward and toward the eventual Punjabi home base.
1986 +
Chandigarh
Settles in Le Corbusier's planned city — the administrative and cultural hub of Punjab and Haryana. Both parents attend Panjab University, where they meet. Ambika has since built a career here as a Lecturer of Biology, spanning over two decades.
Karan Saini
1990s – present
Product Manager
San Francisco
1990s
Born in Chandigarh
Born in the city that represents the consolidation of two generations of internal migration — Chandigarh is both endpoint and launchpad.
2010s
Delhi
Studies at the University of Delhi, then stays on for early professional work in the capital.
2010s
Chicago, United States
Graduate studies at Northwestern University — the family's first international move since the great-grandfather's posting to Cameroon, roughly 75 years earlier.
Present
San Francisco, California
Works as a Product Manager in the technology industry. A journey that began in colonial-era Punjab now spans ~120 years, three continents, and many chapters.
